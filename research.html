Current Research:
Dark Matter Detection Methods in Liquid Helium-4

The McKinsey Research Group, Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory, CA

Summary

I am exploring the use of superfluid liquid helium-4 as a viable candidate for the detection of dark matter particles, specifically weakly interacting massive particles (WIMPS). As opposed to liquid xenon detectors operated by the McKinsey Research Group, currently in use in the LUX and LZ experiments in South Dakota, liquid helium detectors can detect low mass WIMPS using the crystalline structure of the super fluid with much greater precision. My focus is on coding Monte Carlo simulations, using the Python programming language, to create optimality conditions for the detection of light WIMPS using energy kinematics and probabilistic functions.

Under the mentorship of Dr. Scott Hertel, Postdoctoral Associate, and Dr. Daniel McKinsey, Professor of Physics

Dark Matter

As we looked out into the depths of the universe with modern technology, we discovered, astoundingly, that ordinary baryonic matter – the kind that makes up all of the matter we are familiar with, the kind made of protons and neutrons – is not the primary component in our universe. It’s not even half! Rather, almost 85% of the universe is made of this new form of matter yet to be detected. So far, it has been invisible to us, hence the name "dark matter,” but scientists are trying to shed some light on it now. Understanding dark matter could be the key to understanding the construction of our universe, and dictate how forces and interactions play out on colossal scales.

Current Theories and Predictions

The evidence for dark matter came from multiple astronomical observations in the late 20th century. Unlike most massive objects in the night sky, dark matter (unsurprisingly) does not interact strongly with the electromagnetic force, and therefore does not emit light (thereby effectively invisible to us). However, it does have gravitational effects, and multiple observations of these effects, including gravitational lensing and rotational velocity, have given us an introductory glimpse at the properties of dark matter.

Data from multiple events, such primordial nucleosynthesis, cosmic microwave background, structure formation, and microlensing observations, show that dark matter cannot be made of baryons or neutrinos. This implies that the Standard Model of particle physics cannot account for such matter, and therefore new physics must be discovered.

One promising theory currently under investigation is supersymmetry (SUSY). Supersymmetry is a theoretical symmetry between fermions and bosons that allow particles in certain states to have a “superpartner,” and thereby adding new particles to the Standard Model. Using the supersymmetry argument, scientists have theorized the existence of weakly interacting massive particles, or WIMPS, as a likely candidate for dark matter, and many direct detection experiments have now been designed to search for these particles.
